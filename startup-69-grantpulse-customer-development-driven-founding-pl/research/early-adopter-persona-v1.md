# GrantPulse Early Adopter Persona v1
## Phase 1 — Customer Discovery

**Version:** 1.0 | **Domain:** grantshelf.com

---

## How to Use This Document

These personas represent our **early adopter** targets — the people most likely to sign a pilot LOI, try a new tool, and become our first paying customers. They are not exhaustive descriptions of all potential users; they are the specific profiles we're actively recruiting for discovery interviews and pilots.

Update these personas after each batch of 5 interviews.

## Persona 1: "Overwhelmed Sarah"
### Program Officer, Small Community Foundation

**Tagline:** *"I love our grantees. I hate our spreadsheet."*

---

**Demographics**
- Role: Program Officer (often the only program staff)
- Organization: Maplewood Community Foundation (fictitious composite)
- AUM: $1.5–3M
- Team: 2 FTE + 2–3 volunteers
- Grant volume: 60–120 grants/year
- Tenure: 2–6 years in role

**Current Tech Stack**
- Application intake: Google Forms or Typeform
- Tracking: Google Sheets (one master "tracker" with 40+ columns)
- Communication: Gmail (extensive folders and labels system)
- Payments: Manual check requests to finance, occasionally Venmo Business for small amounts
- Reporting: Canva or Google Slides built manually

**A Day in Her Life (Grant Season)**

It's two weeks before the review deadline. Sarah opens the shared Google Sheet to find that a board volunteer has reformatted three columns, breaking her IMPORTRANGE formula. She spends 45 minutes fixing it before she can even start her day. She has 83 applications to process, 6 volunteer reviewers who haven't responded to her last three emails, and a board meeting in 10 days where she needs to present a funding recommendation.

She manually exports the intake form responses, pastes them into the tracker, de-dupes entries (3 duplicates this cycle), removes ineligible applicants (11 of 83 failed the geographic requirement she couldn't enforce in the form), and creates individual scoring sheets for each reviewer. She emails the scoring PDFs. She will follow up with each reviewer individually, 2–4 times, over the next two weeks.

**Top Frustrations (in her words)**

1. *"The tracker spreadsheet is a monster that only I understand."*
2. *"Chasing reviewers takes more time than actually reviewing."*
3. *"Every board meeting I'm building the report from scratch the night before."*
4. *"Our grantee reports are all over the place. I can never tell if we're actually making a difference."*
5. *"I spend about 60% of my time on admin. I got into this work to help people."*

**Goals**
- Run a clean, defensible grant process that the board trusts
- Get some of her time back for relationship-building with grantees
- Produce an annual report she's actually proud of
- Not be the single point of failure when she eventually moves on

**Purchase Behavior**
- Budget authority: ~$1,500/yr discretionary (can spend without board approval)
- Decision process: Her + ED, no board vote needed under $2K
- Buying trigger: "One more chaotic cycle" — she's almost there
- Biggest objection: "What if the data migration is a nightmare?" and "Will this be another tool I'm the only one who knows how to use?"

**Pilot Likelihood:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (highest)  
**Conversion Likelihood (to paid):** ⭐⭐⭐⭐  

**Channels to Reach Her**
- LinkedIn (searches: "community foundation program officer")
- CFLeads network and regional grantmakers associations
- Reddit r/nonprofit (posts with "grant management" frustration)
- Nonprofit Technology Network (NTEN) Slack

**What "Success" Looks Like for Sarah**
- She runs a full grant cycle without a single spreadsheet incident
- Her reviewer coordination goes from 3 weeks to 1 week
- Board prep drops from 6 hours to 45 minutes
- She gets one evening back per week during grant season

## Persona 2: "Careful Carlos"
### Foundation Administrator, Family Foundation

**Tagline:** *"We don't need all the bells and whistles. We need something simple that passes an audit."*

---

**Demographics**
- Role: Foundation Administrator (sole FTE, sometimes part-time)
- Organization: Delgado Family Foundation (fictitious composite)
- AUM: $5–15M
- Team: 1 FTE (him) + Board of 5–7 family members
- Grant volume: 20–50 grants/year
- Tenure: 3–10 years

**Current Tech Stack**
- Was using: Foundant GLM (cancelled — too complex and expensive)
- Now using: Excel for tracking, Outlook for communication, DocuSign for award letters
- Payments: Finance company cuts checks after his email approval
- Reporting: Word document template he built, sent by email

**A Day in His Life**

Carlos's foundation gives about 35 grants a year, all in the arts and education. The board meets quarterly. Every board meeting, Carlos prepares a 20-slide deck from scratch, which takes him 2 full days. He's frustrated because Foundant cost them $800/month for features they used maybe 20% of. He cancelled it 18 months ago and went back to Excel. He's been meaning to find a replacement but hasn't had time to evaluate anything.

His biggest ongoing fear is an audit. The family patriarch (and primary donor) is increasingly asking for documentation that the foundation's grants are "doing what they're supposed to." Carlos doesn't have a clean way to demonstrate grantee outcomes without calling each grantee individually.

He's cautious about new software because the last procurement took 4 months (board approval was required over $5K/yr) and the tool didn't live up to the demo.

**Top Frustrations (in his words)**

1. *"Foundant did too much and charged too much. We're not a large institution."*
2. *"I need to be able to prove our grants are used correctly. I can't do that with email."*
3. *"The board meets once a quarter and I spend two days preparing each time."*
4. *"Donor privacy is real for us — the family doesn't want grant decisions public."*
5. *"I need something that's still standing when the next administrator takes over."*

**Goals**
- Pass the next audit without a finding
- Reduce board prep from 2 days to 2 hours
- Have a clean paper trail for every grant decision and disbursement
- Not overpay for complexity he doesn't need

**Purchase Behavior**
- Budget authority: Can approve <$2,500/yr without board vote (anything larger requires board)
- Decision process: Him + board chair for technology purchases
- Buying trigger: An audit finding, or board asks "can we see better reporting?"
- Biggest objection: "Will the board actually approve this?" and "What if the next person doesn't use it?"

**Pilot Likelihood:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐  
**Conversion Likelihood (to paid):** ⭐⭐⭐⭐  

**Channels to Reach Him**
- Council on Foundations member directory
- LinkedIn (search: "family foundation administrator")
- NCFP (National Center for Family Philanthropy) resources and community
- Regional association of grantmakers

**What "Success" Looks Like for Carlos**
- Next audit produces zero findings related to grant documentation
- Board prep time cut by 75%
- He can answer any donor question about a specific grant in <5 minutes
- He has a successor-ready system that documents institutional knowledge

## Persona 3: "Civic Maya"
### Grants Coordinator, Municipal Government

**Tagline:** *"If I could put this on a grant budget line, I'd do it tomorrow. But procurement is its own monster."*

---

**Demographics**
- Role: Grants Coordinator (or Community Development Specialist)
- Organization: City of Riverside Community Development Office (fictitious composite)
- Program size: $200K–$1M annual grant pool (CDBG, local arts, small business)
- Team: 1–2 FTE on grants + city finance department
- Grant volume: 20–60 grants/year
- Tenure: 2–8 years

**Current Tech Stack**
- Application intake: Submittable (if funded) or Google Forms
- Tracking: Excel + SharePoint (city-managed, slow, frustrating)
- Communication: Outlook (city-managed, strict security policies)
- Payments: Manual check requests through city finance, 3–6 week processing
- Reporting: Excel + Word, submitted to state or federal agencies annually

**A Day in Her Life**

Maya runs the city's community development grant program. She loves the mission but the constraints are exhausting. Every purchase requires IT review, a procurement process, and department head approval — minimum 3 months, often 6–9. Her Excel tracker is on SharePoint, which means it's slow, version-controlled, and accessible only from her city laptop.

She's been asked to justify her program to the city council three times in the last two years. Each time, she rebuilds her impact data manually from grant files and emails. She'd love a better tool but she knows the procurement journey will be painful. Her workaround: she's tried to get grant administration tools funded as a direct program expense in CDBG budgets, but it hasn't been approved.

Her instinct is to start with something free and prove value, then make the case for procurement.

**Top Frustrations (in her words)**

1. *"Our payment process takes 6 weeks. Grantees are starting to lose trust in us."*
2. *"Every state report is manually reformatted from Excel. It takes a week and it's soul-crushing."*
3. *"I have to go through IT security review for every new software tool. Even free ones."*
4. *"The city council wants impact data but I can't aggregate what I have."*
5. *"I work in a silo. There's no one else who knows how our grant program works."*

**Goals**
- Shorten disbursement cycle from 6 weeks to 2 weeks
- Produce defensible state/federal compliance reports without a week of manual work
- Build a case for continued program funding with real impact data
- Eventually get off SharePoint + Excel

**Purchase Behavior**
- Budget authority: Essentially none — all purchases go through procurement
- Decision process: IT security review → procurement → department head → budget cycle
- Buying trigger: Audit finding, CDBG compliance pressure, or finding a way to fund tool via program budget
- Biggest objection: "I'd have to go through procurement" and "IT will say no without a security review"

**Pilot Likelihood:** ⭐⭐⭐ (moderate — free tier is key)  
**Conversion Likelihood (to paid):** ⭐⭐ (long sales cycle; may need a different path)  

**Channels to Reach Her**
- Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA) network
- NLC (National League of Cities) grants community
- CDBG administrators listservs and HUD training events
- LinkedIn (search: "community development grants coordinator")

**What "Success" Looks Like for Maya**
- She reduces disbursement cycle by at least 30%
- Her next state/federal compliance report is generated in hours, not days
- She can show city council an impact dashboard at the next budget hearing
- She builds a case for procurement of the full tool
