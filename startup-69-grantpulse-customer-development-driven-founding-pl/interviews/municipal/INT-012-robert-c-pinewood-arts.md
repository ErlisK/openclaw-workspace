## Key Excerpts

**On the application process:**
> "We still use a PDF application form. Applicants print it, fill it out by hand or in Acrobat, and email it to me. I print them, put them in folders for reviewers, deliver the folders, wait, collect them, scan scores. I know this is absurd. Every year I ask for a budget line for software and every year it comes back zero."

**On the review committee:**
> "Our arts review panel is all community volunteers — artists, arts educators, a retired gallery owner. They're wonderful people and they're not going to use any software I put in front of them. I need a system that accommodates paper scoring — or at least makes it very, very simple."

**On the city council presentation:**
> "After our grant cycle, I present to the city arts commission. They love the stories — who we funded, what they created. But the mayor's office has started asking for 'impact metrics.' I have no idea what that means for arts grants. How do you quantify artistic impact? But I'm being asked for it and I don't have a system to collect it."

**On what he'd pay:**
> "If it was under my departmental discretionary budget — which is $2,000 a year for non-personnel — I could do it without going to procurement. That's the magic number. Above that and I need approval and a justification memo."

**On the pilot opportunity:**
> "Our next grant cycle opens in October for spring awards. I'd pilot something then. The stakes are lower than CDBG and my commission chair has been asking me to modernize the process for two years."

## Debrief Notes

**Key finding:** PDF + physical folder workflow still exists in 2024. Paper-based reviewer process is real.

**Budget signal:** $2,000/year discretionary before procurement kicks in — similar to other small municipal programs.

**Arts grant context:** "Impact metrics for arts grants" — there's an emerging pressure from city leadership to quantify arts impact that arts programs are not equipped to handle. Potential specialized reporting need.
