## Key Excerpts

**On confidentiality:**
> "This is the most important thing: absolute confidentiality. People who apply to our fund are coming from a place of need and often vulnerability. They trust us completely. I cannot use any system where their name and the reason they asked for help could be visible to someone they know in our congregation. Privacy is not a nice-to-have — it's a dealbreaker."

**On the review process:**
> "Our review committee is three people: me, our social worker, and one lay leader. We discuss cases by phone. We never write down names in email — I use case numbers. This means our records are very thin. If someone asked to audit our process, the documentation would not be good."

**On disbursements:**
> "We write a check from our charitable account. I handle this. The check goes to the person or directly to the utility company or landlord. We keep a log in Excel. But the log just says 'check #412, $650, date.' It doesn't connect back to the application or any approval record."

**On what they need:**
> "Something that is absolutely private — role-based access, no one can see cases who shouldn't. And free. We give every dollar to people in need. Software budget is zero."

## Debrief Notes

**Unique privacy requirement:** Congregational fund — applicant identity and reason for need must be hidden from congregation members.

**This is a distinct use case from donor privacy (DAF).** Applicant privacy = people can't know who applied, not just who funded.

**H13 extension:** Role-based access needed not just for donor privacy but also applicant privacy in sensitive contexts.

**New hypothesis H27:** Applicant-facing privacy controls (case numbers, limited visibility) are required for funds serving vulnerable populations.
