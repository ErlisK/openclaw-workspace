## Transcript (Condensed with Key Quotes)

**INTERVIEWER:** Tell me about your role and a typical grant cycle.

**SARAH:** I'm the program officer and I basically am the grants department. We have an ED who does high-level strategy and donor relations, and then there's me. We have some volunteers who help with review but I coordinate everything. A cycle runs from when we open applications to when we get final reports back, so about 6 months, but I'm actively in it for maybe 4 of those months.

**INTERVIEWER:** Walk me through what happens when an application comes in.

**SARAH:** So applicants fill out a Google Form. The responses go into a spreadsheet automatically — that part's actually fine. The problem starts when I have to do anything with that data. I need to check eligibility, I need to remove duplicates — we always get a few — I need to assign applications to reviewers, and I need to keep track of what stage each application is in. And all of that is just... me, manually, in a spreadsheet with 60 columns.

**INTERVIEWER:** 60 columns?

**SARAH:** *(laughs)* It's grown over five years. I've added a column every time something breaks and I needed a workaround. My predecessor built the original and she's long gone, so I've just been adding to it. I genuinely don't know what about 8 of the columns are for.

**INTERVIEWER:** What happens when someone else needs to use it?

**SARAH:** Our ED tried to update it once and broke my IMPORTRANGE formula. I spent an hour fixing it. Now I just... don't let anyone else touch it. Which is a problem because if I get hit by a bus, the whole program stops.

**INTERVIEWER:** Tell me about reviewer coordination.

**SARAH:** We have 8 volunteer reviewers. I email them the scoring rubric as a PDF, they print it, fill it out by hand or type in a Word doc, and email it back. I then copy all the scores into a master spreadsheet. Last cycle it took me four and a half hours to just enter and double-check scores.

**INTERVIEWER:** How long does the review period take?

**SARAH:** The review window is three weeks but I spend the first two weeks chasing people. I send at least 3 reminder emails per reviewer. Last cycle one reviewer was 11 days late submitting scores because they were on vacation and they forgot to tell me they were going. We had to extend the whole timeline.

**INTERVIEWER:** Have you had any conflict-of-interest issues?

**SARAH:** We had one. A reviewer scored an application from an organization she sits on the board of. She didn't disclose it. We didn't catch it until after the awards were announced. We ended up funding that org — the application was actually strong — but the appearance of it was terrible. The ED was not happy. We now have a form people sign but it's still just a PDF I email out.

**INTERVIEWER:** What about disbursements?

**SARAH:** We work with our finance person, who is part-time. I send her an email when someone needs to be paid and she cuts a check. Or we do ACH through our bank portal which requires her to do it manually. There's no tracking on my end — I have a column in my spreadsheet that says "paid" or "pending" and I update it when she tells me. Last cycle she sent a check to the wrong address because we had an old address in her system that she didn't check against my spreadsheet. The check was lost. It took three weeks to figure out what happened and reissue.

**INTERVIEWER:** What do you do with grantee final reports?

**SARAH:** *(sighs)* This is the worst part. They come in every format imaginable. Some are Word docs, some are Google Docs, some people send PDFs, two people last cycle sent a PowerPoint. One person sent a voice memo. I am not joking. I had to transcribe it. 

Our board wants an impact summary. Last spring I spent six days — actual working days — pulling together a summary of outcomes from 85 reports. I had to manually copy numbers out of each report because there's no consistent structure. And after all that, I still couldn't do anything statistically meaningful because the metrics varied so much.

**INTERVIEWER:** How do you prepare for board meetings?

**SARAH:** The week before a board meeting is hell week. I build a 15-slide deck, I pull grant stats manually, I build a bar chart in Google Sheets, I copy it into slides, it breaks, I redo it. I was up until midnight the night before our last board meeting. That's just... that shouldn't be how this works.

**INTERVIEWER:** On a 1-10 scale, how severe is the spreadsheet / admin problem?

**SARAH:** For me personally? Nine. Maybe nine and a half. I got into this work because I care about the grantees and the community impact. I spend maybe 55-60% of my time on admin. That's not okay. If I could get that down to 25%, I could spend so much more time actually supporting our grantees. That's the work I want to be doing.

**INTERVIEWER:** Have you looked at any tools to solve this?

**SARAH:** We looked at Submittable two years ago. The intake and review features were great. But it didn't really have post-award management — like, the disbursement tracking and milestone stuff just wasn't there. And the pricing was way out of range. I don't remember the exact number but it was something like $12,000 a year or a "let us build you a proposal" situation. Our entire software budget is $1,800 a year.

**INTERVIEWER:** What would it look like if this problem went away?

**SARAH:** I'd have time to do site visits. I'd have time to actually read the grantee reports instead of just mining them for data points. I'd be able to build relationships with applicants who didn't get funded and help them improve their applications. That's the work. Right now I'm just a spreadsheet jockey.

**INTERVIEWER:** If a tool existed that covered your whole grant cycle for a price that worked — what would make you trust it enough to switch?

**SARAH:** I'd need to know my data was safe, that I could get it out if the company disappeared. I'd want to see it working for another org like ours first. And honestly, I'd need to be able to try it in a low-stakes cycle before committing. The switching cost isn't the data — it's the learning curve during a cycle. If I'm fumbling with a new tool while I'm supposed to be reviewing applications, that's a disaster.

**INTERVIEWER:** Pricing-wise — what range would feel right?

**SARAH:** If it replaced everything — spreadsheet, email coordination, payment tracking, reporting — honestly I could justify $1,500 a year to my ED without a board vote. If it had everything you're describing, I'd fight for $2,500. Anything over $3,000 and I need board approval and that's a 3-month process minimum.

**INTERVIEWER:** Would you be interested in being a pilot user?

**SARAH:** Yes. If it's free to try and I can get my data out anytime, absolutely. Our next cycle opens in September. If you could have something for me to test by then, I'm in.
