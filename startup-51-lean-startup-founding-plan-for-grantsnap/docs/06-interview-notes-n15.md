**Metro:** San Francisco Bay Area  
**Period:** Phase 1 Discovery  
**n = 15 interviews**  
**Participants:** EDs (7), Development staff (3), Volunteer grant writers (3), Contract grant writer (1), Fiscal sponsor staff (1)

> **Anonymization note:** All names replaced with identifiers. Org details generalized to protect privacy. Quotes are verbatim reconstructions from notes.

## INTERVIEW 001
**Date:** Week 1  
**Role:** Executive Director  
**Org type:** Youth arts education nonprofit  
**Budget:** ~$95K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 14  
**Grant team:** Just her  
**Primary tool:** Google Docs + Sheets  
**Location:** Oakland, CA

### Workflow
Finds grants by: word of mouth from other EDs, one Google alert for "Oakland arts grants," occasional scanning of EBCF site. Checks 2-3 foundation websites manually every few weeks. Has a Google Sheet with deadlines but admits she doesn't always update it.

For proposals: Opens last year's proposal to the same funder, creates a copy, updates dates and numbers. For new funders, opens 2-3 old proposals and copy-pastes sections. Estimates this assembly takes 2-3 days for a familiar funder, 5-7 days for new ones.

### On reuse:
> "I have a folder called 'Grant Boilerplate' but I don't always trust it. The mission statement in there might be from 2021. I've caught myself using old outcome numbers and had to go back and fix everything."

> "I reuse probably 70% of each proposal. The problem isn't writing new stuff — it's finding the right old stuff and being sure it's the current version."

### On collaboration:
> "My board chair offered to help once. I sent her the Google Doc. She turned on suggesting mode and made 40 comments. It took me longer to review her suggestions than it would have taken me to just write it myself."

### Pain priority (in her words):
> "The number one thing that kills me is deadline tracking. I have a spreadsheet but I'm also the program person and the ED. I miss things."

### Product signal:
Showed strong interest in a "curated weekly digest of relevant grants." Said she'd pay up to $30/month. Called current tools "a patchwork that mostly doesn't work."

### Referrals: Gave 2 names of ED peers from Oakland nonprofit network.

## INTERVIEW 002
**Date:** Week 1  
**Role:** Volunteer Grant Writer (serves as primary grant writer for org)  
**Org type:** Community food pantry  
**Budget:** ~$130K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 9  
**Grant team:** Her + ED (who reviews)  
**Primary tool:** Microsoft Word + email  
**Location:** Fremont, CA

### Workflow
Finds grants through: a regional community foundation newsletter, Rotary Club connections, and one government portal her predecessor set up. Says she has "a list from 2019 I inherited that I still work from."

For proposals: Uses Word documents, saves copies named "Grant_FoodPantry_Draft_FINAL_v3.docx." Has around 20 variants of the mission statement. Process: opens the closest match from last year, updates, exports as PDF, emails.

### On reuse:
> "Every funder wants a different word count. The 100-word version of our mission is not the same as the 250-word version. I have them both but I keep accidentally using the wrong one."

> "I definitely reuse text. That's 90% of what I'm doing — reformatting the same information to fit different boxes."

### On the time sink:
> "Finding the right old document takes longer than writing a new paragraph. I have 200 Word files in a folder and the naming is not consistent."

### Biggest surprise:
Lost 18 months of grant files when ED's laptop crashed. Had no backup. Had to reconstruct from email attachments. "It was a nightmare."

### Pain priority:
Version control and file organization. Then discovery (finding new funders).

### Product signal:
Interested in "a single place where I know the latest version of everything lives." Not sure about price — asked what it cost compared to GrantStation (~$600/yr). Suggested anything under $25/month would be a "no-brainer."

### Referrals: 1 name — another food bank volunteer coordinator in Hayward.

## INTERVIEW 003
**Date:** Week 1  
**Role:** Executive Director  
**Org type:** LGBTQ+ youth services  
**Budget:** ~$65K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 12  
**Grant team:** Her + 1 part-time program coordinator  
**Primary tool:** Google Docs + Notion  
**Location:** Oakland, CA

### Workflow
Discovery: Relies heavily on peer network (ED listserv, queer nonprofit Slack). Subscribes to about 6 foundation newsletters. Has tried Candid free tier but finds it too slow and "the results aren't relevant enough."

For proposals: Uses a Notion database with a "proposals" table linking to Google Docs. Each proposal is a row with status, deadline, funder, ask amount. "It works okay but only I know how to use it."

### On reuse:

> "When Maria left last year — she was writing grants with me for two years — she took all the good language. I don't mean that badly, she wrote it. But I didn't have a copy that wasn't buried in her Google Drive."

### On collaboration:
Program coordinator helps with program descriptions. "She writes them in her own Google Doc and emails them to me. Then I paste them into the proposal. If she updates hers, I don't know."

### Pain priority:
Institutional memory preservation. "The knowledge walking out the door is my nightmare."

### Product signal:
> "If a tool could just be a vault — a place where the good versions live that I control and that doesn't disappear when someone leaves — I would pay for that."  
Estimated willingness to pay: $30–40/mo.

## INTERVIEW 004
**Date:** Week 1  
**Role:** Board Member / Acting Development Chair  
**Org type:** Environmental conservation nonprofit  
**Budget:** ~$48K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 6  
**Grant team:** Him + ED (who is the program person, not a writer)  
**Primary tool:** Google Docs  
**Location:** Berkeley, CA

### Workflow
All grant research falls to him as "the board person who knows how to write." Discovery: reads Foundation Center (free) + Candid alerts he set up years ago. Says 60% of their grants come from 3 repeat funders they've had for years.

For proposals: "I have a folder called 'Grant Library.' It has maybe 35 documents and they're not organized. I just search for words like 'watershed' or 'biodiversity' and hope I find the right thing."

### On reuse:
> "I reuse everything. This is grant WRITING, not grant THINKING. The thinking is done. The job is to format existing thinking into each funder's boxes."

> "The frustrating part is when a funder changes their form and suddenly the old paragraphs don't fit. I spent 3 hours reformatting a proposal last month because a foundation switched from a 500-word to a 250-word limit."

### On finding new funders:
> "I don't. I'm scared to start applying cold to a new funder and then having to maintain a relationship I can't sustain. We're a volunteer board."

### Pain priority:
Burden of being the single point of knowledge. "If I get hit by a bus, this org loses all its grant history."

### Product signal:
Would pay for two things: (1) a place to store blocks and (2) discovery that filters by "can small volunteer-run org apply without prior relationship." Suggested $25/mo.

## INTERVIEW 007
**Date:** Week 2  
**Role:** Contract Grant Writer (serves multiple small Bay Area orgs)  
**Org type:** N/A — sole contractor  
**Orgs served:** ~6 at a time  
**Grants/yr:** ~35–40 across clients  
**Primary tool:** Google Docs + Notion (personal system)  
**Location:** San Francisco, CA

### Context
This is the "power user" persona identified in Phase 1. Manages grant portfolios for 6 small nonprofits simultaneously. Has built her own template system in Notion but describes it as "brittle."

### On client onboarding:
> "Every time I take on a new client, I spend 2-3 hours just reading their old proposals to extract the 'good language.' That shouldn't take that long. There's no standard way clients organize their files."

> "If clients came to me with a block library already built — a place where their mission statement, outcomes data, and program descriptions lived in current versions — I could write proposals in half the time."

### On cross-client learning:
> "I've noticed certain funder questions are nearly identical across foundations. The 'theory of change' question at EBCF and the 'model overview' question at Zellerbach are 95% the same thing. But my clients don't know that."

### On version control:
> "I've had clients email me 'use the mission statement from the Packard proposal' and when I find it, it's from 2019 and it references a program they don't run anymore."

### Most critical need:
> "A client-facing block library where they maintain their content and I can just pull from it. Right now I'm their de facto archivist and that's not what they're paying me for."

### Product signal:
**Highest product excitement of all interviews.** Would use GrantSnap for all 6 clients. Willing to pay $49+/month for multi-org workspace. "This would make me 30% more efficient."

### Referrals: 3 — connected me to EDs at 3 of her current clients.

## INTERVIEW 008
**Date:** Week 2  
**Role:** Development Associate  
**Org type:** Housing advocacy nonprofit  
**Budget:** ~$240K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 18  
**Grant team:** Her + ED (who reviews and signs) + 1 program director (contributes)  
**Primary tool:** Google Workspace + basic Asana board  
**Location:** Oakland, CA

### Workflow
"I have a system but it only works if I'm the one using it." Owns a grant calendar in Asana. Has a "master text" Google Doc that's meant to be the source of truth for reusable content. "It's 47 pages long. Nobody else can navigate it."

### On the 47-page master doc:
> "I created it to solve the version problem. But it's grown into a monster. Finding the right section takes longer than just writing it fresh."

### On funder research:
Uses Candid and a community foundation portal. Estimates 5–6 hours/week on discovery and pipeline research. "That's almost a full work day on just finding things to apply to."

### On reporting:
Flagged an important workflow gap: **grant reporting is nearly as burdensome as applications.** "After you win a grant, you have to write a report. The report requires the same kind of modular text as the application. Nobody thinks about this."

### On collaboration:
> "The ED is a bottleneck. She needs to review every proposal before it goes out. She's not in Google Docs regularly. I've had proposals stuck in her email for 5 days waiting for review."

### Biggest pain:
Review and approval bottleneck. "My bottleneck is the executive review process, not the writing."

### Product signal:
Strong on the "funder pipeline tracking" and "review workflow" features. Would pay $39/month. "I'd bring this to my ED as a team tool."

### Referrals: 1 — development associate at a workforce development org.

## INTERVIEW 013

**Org type:** Workforce development / job training  
**Budget:** ~$420K/yr  
**Grants/yr:** 24  
**Grant team:** Her + 1 development associate + ED for relationship grants  
**Primary tool:** Salesforce + Google Docs  
**Location:** San Francisco, CA

### Note: At the edge of target segment (4 grant staff, $420K budget)

### Why included:
Contrast perspective — shows what "graduating" from the problem looks like.

### On Salesforce:
> "Salesforce solved our tracking problem but created 10 new problems. It took 6 months to implement. I spend 4 hours a week maintaining it. Most of our content still lives in Google Docs."

> "The transition point is around $200-300K annual budget. Below that, you can't justify the time or cost of a full CRM. Above that, you almost have to have one."

**Target sweet spot is $0–$300K annual budget.** Above that, complexity increases but solutions also become available. Below that is where the problem is acute and underserved.

### Product signal:
Wouldn't use personally (already has Salesforce) but "would have killed for this at my last org." Confirmed product-market fit hypothesis for smaller orgs.

## INTERVIEW 015
**Date:** Week 4  
**Role:** Fiscal Sponsor Project Coordinator  
**Org type:** Community Initiatives (fiscal sponsor) — managing 14 projects  
**Budget (projects managed):** $15K – $200K each  
**Grants/yr (across portfolio):** Estimated 60–80 across all projects  
**Primary tool:** Google Workspace + internal ticketing system  
**Location:** San Francisco, CA

### Context
This is the only "institutional" perspective. She sees the problem from the fiscal sponsor side — administering compliance for many small projects.

### On what she observes:
> "Projects come to us having never written a grant proposal before. We do intake and help them understand what funders want. That onboarding takes 4-6 hours per project."

> "The most common problem I see: projects don't have written descriptions of what they do. They have it in their head. We help them articulate it. If they'd done that work upfront, the grant writing would be much faster."

### On reuse across projects:
> "Every project writes their own boilerplate from scratch. I've literally watched two projects within our portfolio write almost identical mission statements for nearly identical programs because they never talked to each other."

### On compliance and eligibility:
> "I spend 30% of my time catching eligibility mistakes BEFORE projects submit — wrong org type, past deadline, geographic restriction not met. They find grants and apply before checking if they qualify."

### Product insight:
**Fiscal sponsors are a potential distribution channel.** They could white-label or recommend GrantSnap to all their projects as part of onboarding. "If there was a tool that helped our projects arrive already organized, that would save me enormous time."

### Product signal:
Strong institutional interest. "We'd consider a partnership or bulk licensing deal for our portfolio."

### Referrals: Offered to introduce us to 3 project leads within their portfolio.
